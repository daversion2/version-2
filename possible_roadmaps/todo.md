Missing CBT Techniques
1. REFLECTIONS Thought Records / ABC Model
The classic CBT worksheet: Activating event → Belief → Consequence → Dispute → Effect. Your app captures the negative story and inner voice response once during onboarding, but doesn't provide an ongoing structured way to log and challenge distorted thoughts in real-time when they arise.

Journaling exercise - sit in silience and pay attention to thoughts and label?

2. REFLECTIONS Cognitive Distortion Labeling
You store a cognitive_distortions field on goals, but there's no feature that teaches users to name their distortions (all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, mind reading, fortune telling, emotional reasoning, should statements, etc.). Labeling creates psychological distance from the thought.

This is maybe when someone fails something, they get prompted to do this?
Or it can be part of daily journals

3. REFLECTIONS Behavioral Chain Analysis
When a user fails/skips, there's no structured breakdown of: trigger → thought → emotion → urge → behavior → consequence. This is more granular than your current trigger identification and helps users see the full sequence so they can intervene earlier.


5. REFLECTIONS Mood Tracking & Mood-Behavior Connection
No current mechanism for users to log their emotional state and correlate it with follow-through. CBT heavily emphasizes the thought-feeling-behavior triangle. Even a simple mood check-in before/after challenges would add this dimension.


6. Downward Arrow Technique
Drilling deeper into beliefs: "If that were true, what would it mean?" → repeated until you reach the core belief. Useful for uncovering deeper schemas beyond surface-level negative stories.
--> maybe a separate exercise?

7. Evidence For/Against (Balanced Thinking)
A structured exercise where users list evidence for and against their negative belief, then write a balanced alternative thought. More rigorous than the current inner voice challenge/response.

8. Cost-Benefit Analysis of Behaviors
A structured pros/cons matrix for both continuing the old behavior AND adopting the new one. Helps with ambivalence (common in diet, phone, social media goals).

9. Pleasant Activity Scheduling
Explicitly scheduling enjoyable activities to counteract low mood or reward completion. Your programs have structured activities, but there's no general feature for scheduling rewards/pleasant activities as part of the behavior plan.

10. SMART Goal Decomposition
While you have goals with target dates, there's no structured Specific-Measurable-Achievable-Relevant-Time-bound breakdown. The "minimum action" covers some of this, but a more rigorous decomposition could help.

11. Scaling Questions (0-10 Progress)
"Where are you now on a 0-10 scale? What would one point higher look like?" Used periodically (not just at baseline) to track subjective progress and identify concrete next steps.

13. Self-Compassion / Compassionate Mind Training
When users fail or have low follow-through, there's no structured self-compassion exercise (e.g., "What would you say to a friend in this situation?"). Your recovery plan touches this but isn't explicitly compassion-focused.

14. Successive Approximation (Shaping)
Breaking overwhelming tasks into the tiniest possible first step. Related to your "minimum action" but could be more systematic — especially when users report low confidence.

Highest-Impact Additions (My Recommendations)
If I were prioritizing, I'd suggest these three as most impactful for your app's model:

Thought Records — gives users an ongoing CBT tool, not just a one-time onboarding exercise
Cognitive Distortion Labeling — you already store the field; build the teaching/identification UI
Behavioral Chain Analysis on failure — turns missed days into learning moments rather than just "failure reflections"
These three would transform the app from setting up CBT-informed goals to actively practicing CBT as an ongoing skill.
