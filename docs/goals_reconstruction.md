# Goal Creation Flow — Neuro-Nudge

---

## Screen 1 — What's the goal?

**Prompt:** *"What do you want to achieve?"*

Below the input, a framing note before they type: *"Be specific and realistic. This is proven to increase the probability of achieving goals."*

A subtle SMART checklist can appear beneath their text as they type — soft checkmarks that light up as their input includes a measurable element, a timeframe, etc. Not blocking, just encouraging.

> 🧠 **Neuroscience note:** Specific goals create a well-defined "gap" between current state and desired state. The brain's dopaminergic system fires in response to closing that gap — but only if the gap is concrete enough to perceive. Vague goals produce no gap signal, and therefore no motivational pull.

---

## Screen 2 — Why does this matter to you?

**Prompt:** *"What changes in your life when you achieve this?"*

As soon as they type anything in the field, a soft blurb appears beneath: *"Is there anything underneath this reason that's more fundamental? The deeper the why, the stronger the motivation."* It stays visible as a gentle reminder — not a required second field, just an invitation to go deeper before moving on.

> 🧠 **Neuroscience note:** Goals anchored to intrinsic values — identity, growth, meaning — activate the limbic system's motivational circuitry far more durably than surface-level reasons. Extrinsic reasons (money, approval) produce motivation that fades; values-based reasons self-renew.

---

## Screen 3 — Who are you becoming?

**Prompt:** *"Finish this sentence:"*

A large, clean fill-in-the-blank display:

> **"By achieving this goal, I am becoming someone who ___________"**

The blank is tappable and opens a text input. No pre-population — they write it fresh. Keep the screen minimal so the statement feels like a declaration, not a form field.

> 🧠 **Neuroscience note:** The brain's self-concept is one of its most powerful behavioral regulators. When a goal is framed as an expression of who you're becoming rather than something you're doing, it recruits the default mode network's identity-maintenance circuitry — you start to act in ways that are consistent with the person you've declared yourself to be.

---

## Screen 4 — How will you measure success?

**Prompt:** *"How will you know you've achieved it?"*

Four tappable cards:

| Icon | Type | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| ✓ | **Done by a date** | A one-time finish line |
| # | **Reach a number** | A metric you'll hit over time |
| ↑ | **Hit a total** | Accumulate X instances |
| ★ | **Rate yourself** | Weekly reflection, no hard metric |

> 🧠 **Neuroscience note:** Measurable goals allow the brain to continuously calculate progress. This activates a feedback loop in the striatum — the closer you get, the more motivating the goal becomes. Without measurement, there's no feedback loop and motivation stays flat.

---

## Screen 5 — Measurement config

*Adapts dynamically based on Screen 4 selection.*

- **Done by a date** → Date picker only
- **Reach a number** → Metric name, starting value, target value, direction (up/down)
- **Hit a total** → Target count, optional deadline
- **Rate yourself** → Scale choice (1–5 or 1–10), check-in day of week, and: *"What question will you ask yourself each week?"* — default: *"How well did I live this goal this week?"* — this question surfaces in nightly reflection

*No neuroscience blurb on this screen — pure configuration. Keep it clean and fast.*

---

## Screen 6 — Name the obstacle

**Prompt:** *"What's the most likely thing that gets in your way?"*

Free text. One honest answer.

After they type, a soft blurb appears below:

> *"Now close your eyes for a moment. Picture yourself hitting this obstacle. Feel the friction of it. Then picture yourself pushing through it anyway — what does that look like?"*

A small **"I've visualized it"** button confirms they've done it before advancing. Not gating — a mindful pause that gives the visualization instruction gravity.

> 🧠 **Neuroscience note:** Pure positive visualization of success paradoxically reduces follow-through — it gives the brain a premature sense of completion. Mental contrasting, developed by psychologist Gabriele Oettingen, requires holding both the desired future and the real obstacle in mind simultaneously. This keeps motivational tension active and primes the brain to recognize and respond to the obstacle when it actually appears.

---

## Screen 7 — Your plan

Show their obstacle from Screen 6 in a soft callout card, then:

**Prompt:** *"When [their obstacle], I will…"*

The "When" clause is pre-filled from Screen 6. They complete the "I will" portion.

Below, a second optional field:

> *"And if that's not possible in the moment, my minimum action is…"*

This captures the floor behavior — not just the ideal response.

> 🧠 **Neuroscience note:** Implementation intentions — "if X, then Y" plans — have been shown in meta-analyses to roughly double goal follow-through rates. By pre-deciding the response, you offload the decision from willpower to procedural memory. When the obstacle arrives, the brain doesn't deliberate — it executes.

---

## Screen 8 — Commit

Before the summary, one final visualization moment. Full screen, minimal UI:

> *"Take 10 seconds. Picture the version of you who achieved this goal. Not the effort — the arrival. What does that feel like?"*

A subtle 10-second ambient timer, then a gentle transition to the summary card.

**Summary card shows:**
- Goal
- Why
- Identity statement
- Measurement type + config
- Obstacle
- Plan

**Primary CTA:** "I'm committing to this."
**Secondary:** "Save as draft."

> 🧠 **Neuroscience note:** The act of committing publicly or in writing activates self-perception theory — we come to believe what we declare, especially when we've invested time and reflection to get there. The visualization beforehand primes the brain's reward circuitry with a concrete emotional target, making the commitment feel earned rather than obligatory.

---

*Full arc: clarify → anchor in values → claim an identity → define measurement → anticipate resistance → plan through it → commit with feeling.*
